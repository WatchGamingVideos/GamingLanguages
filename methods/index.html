Books and Sites about Language Learning Methods

(Books are not listed in any particular order.)

Kleinschroth: Sprachen Lernen

Kleinschroth, Robert: Sprachen lernen: Der Schl√ºssel zur richtigen Technik. Reinbek bei Hamburg: Rowohlt Taschenbuch Verlag, 2000 (2012).

This book describes techniques for learning vocabulary and grammar, and for improving reading skills, writing skills, listening skilss and oral skills.

Wyner: Fluent Forever

Wyner, Gabriel: Fluent Forever: How to Learn Any Language Fast and Never Forget It. New York: Harmony Books, 2014.

This book explains a lot of cognitive psychology that is relevant to learning in general and learning languages in particular. It also gives very practical advices about techniques (e.g. spaced repetition) and tools.
The accompanying website provides lots of materials that complement the book.

Fluent in 3 Months

Lewis, Benny: Fluent in 3 Months: How Anyone at Any Age Can Learn to Speak Any Language from Anywhere in the World. HarperCollins, 2014 (256 p.).

Benny Lewis is an Irishman who did not enjoy learning languages before reaching adulthood, when he started looking for more efficient and enjoyable methods. The result is the above book, a website, a TEDx talk and a YouTube channel (and another YouTube channel). Some of his blog posts are about language hacks; some of these blog posts focus on specific languages, while others are more general.
His most important piece of advice is to start speaking from the first day instead of worrying about mistakes. Some language learners find that Benny Lewis' method is something for extroverts. Lewis also wrote a controversial blog post titled The Shy Delusion (claiming, for example, that only 1% of people are introverts), which drew some criticism, for example on the website Fluency for Introverts.

The Linguist (Steve Kaufman)

Steve Kaufman is a Canadian who spoke only English until the age of 17, but who can now speak twelve languages. He is co-founder of LingQ, an online language learning system and Web 2.0 community, whose members from all over the world help each other learn up to 21 languages. His blog The Linguist on Language covers many aspects of language learning: techniques, tools, activities, motivation, hacks for specific languages (e.g. for Chinese), etcetera. His YouTube channel LingoSteve's Language Corner hosts the videos embedded in his blog posts but these can be watched independently of those blog posts. One of his playlists is called The Seven Secrets of Language Learning and is worth watching if you are new to learning foreign languages or if you are frustrated by slow progress.

The Polyglot Dream

The Polyglot Dream is a website by the Italian polyglot Luca Lampariello, who claims fluency in 10 languages. His website offers strategies, not hacks, for learning languages. The central concept of his method is his focus on developing a solid language core: the ability to put words and sounds together in order to talk to people about what interests me in a natural way. He also has a YouTube channel; one of his playlists is about language learning strategies, another is about phonetics.

Alexander Arguelles

Foreign Language Expertise is a site by Alexander Arguelles, a linguistics professor from the USA who has devoted his life to studying languages. He can read over thirty languages (including older ones such as Old Norse, Middle Dutch and Ancient Greek). He also has a YouTube channel. He discusses a few language learning techniques on his website and in his “Methodology” playlist.

Becoming Fluent

Roberts, Richard M.; Kreuz, Roger J.: Becoming Fluent: How Cognitive Science Can Help Adults Learn a Foreign Language. MIT Press, 2015. (248 p.) ISBN 9780262029230 (paper) / 9780262330459 (e-book).

Brown, Roediger & McDaniel: Make It Stick

Brown, Peter C.; Roediger, Henry L. III; McDaniel, Mark A.: Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning. Cambridge, MA, London, England: The Belknap Press of Harvard University Press, 2014 (336 p.).

This book was written by two professors of psychology (Roediger and McDaniel) who teamed up with a novelist. The not specifically about learning languages but about learning in general.

How To Learn a Foreign Language

Fuller, Graham E.: How To Learn a Foreign Language. Storm King Press, 1991. ISBN 0935166025 (102 pages).
Graham Fuller has written mostly about the Middle East and Islam. On the webpage about the book, he describes himself as a language expert (…) whose career has required him to learn more than a dozen languages.

Routines and Study Schedules

  • John Fortheringham: Sample Language Learning Routine (October 2014). This excerpt from Master Japanese describes an “immersion schedule” that can be tweaked to fit any lifestyle or work schedule and can be adapted to other languages.

Language Learning Strategies

Language learning strategies refers to the processes and actions that are consciously deployed by language learners to help them to learn or use a language more effectively (Wikipedia).

  • Center for Advanced Research on Language Acquisition (CARLA): Research on Language Learning Strategies and Styles and Strategies-Based Language Instruction: Selected Bibliography (compiled by Andrew D. Cohen; there are a few links to downloadable PDF files).
  • Ali Cullerton: Effective Strategies for Learning a New Language (YouTube, 52 min., December 2014).
  • Language Institute, University of Wisconsin-Madison: Language Learning Strategies, a course taught to language students by Dianna Murphy in 2009:
    • Part 1: explains what language learning strategies are, asks students what strategies they use (e.g. using flashcards for vocabulary, using the language outside class, practice every day, making associations), types of strategies (metacognitive, affective and cognitive) and discusses student motivations for learning specific languages.
    • Part 2: figuring out what kind of learner you are (learning style: visual, auditory, kinesthetic), what kind of learning tasks you enjoy (depending on where you are on the introverted-extraverted continuum, on the closure-oriented versus open continuum), how you deal with new information (global versus particular), how you commit new material to memory (sharpeners versus leveler), how you deal with language rules (deductive versus inductive).
    • Part 3: the relation between learning styles and learning strategies, “effective” learning strategies (outcomes of research on good learners).
    • Part 4: continues discussion of research on good learners, and discusses the matching of strategies and tasks. (The instructor also recommends the book How to Be a More Successful Language Learner by Joan Rubin and Irene Thompseon (Heinle, 1994).
  • Anna Uhl Chamot: CALLA Content and Language Learning Strategies: a list with metacognitive strategies and task-based strategies,
