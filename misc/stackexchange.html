Stack Exchange is a network of of question & answer sites about specific topics. People can ask questions or answer other people's questions. Both questions and answers can received upvotes and downvotes. Upvotes on answers bring the “best” answers to the top. There are several Stack Exchange sites related to language.

Anyone can propose a new Stack Exchange site. The proposal will then go through several phases before the site can “graduate”: definition (which is mainly about which types of questions would be allowed), commit, and public beta. A beta site needs to acquire a specific critical mass (in users, daily visitors, number of questions per day, number of answers per question) before it can graduate.

“Horizontal” Sites

The first two sites are “horizontal” in the sense that they are not about a specific language but focus on a specific aspect that applies across languages:

  • Linguistics (beta): a question and answer site for professional linguists and others with an interest in linguistic research and theory.
  • Language Learning (beta): a question and answer site for students, teachers, polyglots, and anyone interested in the techniques of second-language acquisition. (In spite of the description, the site is also accepts questions about first-language acquisition.)

Language-Specific Sites

Most language-related sites focus on a specific language. (Sites that have graduated are listed first. The beta sites are listed in alphabetical order.)

  • English Language and Usage: a question and answer site for linguists, etymologists, and serious English language enthusiasts.
  • English Language Learners: a question and answer site for speakers of other languages learning English.
  • Japanese Language: a question and answer site for students, teachers, and linguists wanting to discuss the finer points of the Japanese language.
  • Chinese Language (beta): a question and answer site for students, teachers, and linguists wanting to discuss the finer points of the Chinese language. Questions and answers are typically in English. Some answers are in (Standard) Chinese.
  • Esperanto (public beta since August 2016): a question and answer site for teachers and students of the Esperanto language. Questions are in English or Esperanto.
  • French Language (beta): a question and answer site for students, teachers, and linguists wanting to discuss the finer points of the French language. Questions and answers are in English or French.
  • German Language (beta): a bilingual question and answer site for speakers of all levels who want to share and increase their knowledge of the German language. Questions and answers are in English or German.
  • Italian Language (beta): a question and answer site for students, teachers, and linguists wanting to discuss the finer points of the Italian language. Questions and answers are in English or Italian.
  • Korean Language (beta): Korean Language Stack Exchange is a question and answer site for linguists, teachers and students of the Korean language. Questions and answers are typically in English, but questions and answers entirely in Korean are allowed.
  • Latin Language (beta): a question and answer site for linguists, teachers, and students wanting to discuss the finer points of the Latin language.
  • Portuguese Language (beta): a question and answer site for linguists, teachers and learners wanting to discuss the finer points of the Portuguese language.
  • Russian Language (beta): a question and answer site for students, teachers, and linguists wanting to discuss the finer points of the Russian language. Questions and answers are in English or Russian.
  • Spanish Language (beta): a question and answer site for students, teachers, and linguists wanting to discuss the finer points of the Spanish language

Resources for Researching Language Learning Stack Exchange Questions

Below is a set of resources that may be helpful when researching questions for Language Learning. A modified version of the content below was submitted to a community wiki on Language Learning Stack Exchange.

  • ResearchGate: a social network for researchers. Many researchers upload their papers here.
  • Academia.edu: another social network for researchers.
  • Paperity: describes itself as the first multidisciplinary aggregator of Open Access journals and papers.
  • ERIC: a website that indexes research papers relating to education, including research on second language acquisition. ERIC always provides an abstract but does not always store the paper itself.
  • JSTOR describes itself as an online research and teaching platform that helps people discover, use, and build upon a wide range of content in a trusted digital archive. JSTOR contains many papers but access usually requires a paid subscription. There is also a free registration option that allows you to read up to 3 papers every two weeks.
  • Springer Link: Springer is a big academic publisher. Some of the papers available on Springer Link are available as Open Access publications.
  • Elsevier's SciencDirect: Elsevier is another big academic publisher. Some of the papers available on SciencDirect are available as Open Access publications.
  • Google Scholar: a search engine that indexes scholarly literature.
  • (Microsoft Academic Search probably has few artitcles related to language learning.)
  • Scientillion: search engine for ArXiv.org.
  • Research Connection: its goal is to globally connect students and researchers in a single online community that promotes accessibility and collaboration.
  • WorldCat is the world's largest library catalog. WorldCat helps you locate books in a large number of libraries, so you can find out whether a specific book is available in a (university) library near you.
  • TermSciences: a multidisciplinary terminology portal.
  • See also: Rachel Bruce & Neil Jacobs: Ten search engines for researchers that go beyond Google, JISC blog, 11.07.2013. Recommended resources:
    • WolframAlpha, an “answer engine”.
    • CORE: an open access search engine.
    • BASE: a search engine for open access resources.
    • Copac: meta catalogue or federated search engine for the catalogues of university libraries in the UK and Ireland.
    • Web Scale Discovery services.
    • Zetoc: a research database that covers over 28,000 journals.
    • Euroeana: a meta-catalogue of cultural heritage collections in the European Union.
    • Mendeley: reference manager.
    • Zotero: reference manager.
  • Make Google searches work for you, a guide by JISC in the UK.
  • Satish Chandra Satyarthi: How to use Google Search Better – Some Secret Tricks, The Personal Blog of Satish Chandra Satyarthi, 27.12.2015.
  • For other databases and search engines, see Wikipedia's list of academic databases and search engines.
