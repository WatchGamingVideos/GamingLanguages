Introduction

One of the most difficult aspects of learning Dutch is guessing or memorising the grammatical gender of nouns. There are three genders in Dutch — masculine, feminine and neuter — but only two definite articles, namely de for masculine and feminine nouns and het for neuter nouns. As a consequence, there is no straightforward mapping between definite articles and grammatical gender for masculine and feminine words. (Compare with German, where the definite articles der, die and das are consistently used for masculine, feminine and neuter words, respectively.) In Dutch, the three genders are known as mannelijk / m. (masculine), vrouwelijk / v. (feminine) and onzijdig / o. (neuter).

The Netherlands Versus Flanders

To complicate things, the gender system in the Duch language is not stable: since a few decades there has been a tendency for the masculine and feminine genders to become amalgamated into a common gender. This tendency started in the Netherlands several centuries ago but did not affect Belgian speakers of Dutch, because Flemish dialects helped maintain the distinction between the masculine and feminine gender of nouns (see Heidbuchel & Luyten, 1986: 45; Luyten, 1998: 70). Even in the Netherlands the development of the common gender exhibited geographical differences: in the 1970s, J. P. M. Tacx noted that the disctinction had almost completely disappeared in the west of the country, but not in the east (Tacx, 1972: 110).

As a result of this development, many de words can now be treated as both masculine and feminine. (See mannelijk / vrouwelijk / onzijdig on taaltelefoon.be.) However, this does not imply that the distinction between masculine and feminine has completely disappeared (see below). In addition, the distinction between de words and het words still exists.

The Dutch treat most de words for objects and animals as masculine, except when referring to female animals. In the Netherlands, de kat, de krant, de tafel and de tas are masculine, whereas in Flanders they may still be treated as feminine. In order to acknowledge this difference between the Netherlands and Flanders, dictionaries identified the gender of such words as v. (m.). The Woordenlijst Nederlandse taal of 1995 used de for words that are masculine in the Netherlands and feminine in Flanders; the other categories of nouns were marked with de (m.), de (v.) or het.

Rules for Grammatical Gender

Below are a few categories of words for which the gender and definite article is easy to derive. For all other words, learners will need to memorise the definite article. For plural nouns, the definite article is always de.

Neuter Nouns

When a noun is neuter, this means that you refer to back to it using the pronouns het and zijn.

  • diminutive forms: het meisje, het mannetje, het huisje, het koekje, het boompje, het bedje, het bloempje, het pennetje, het etuitje;
  • substantivised infinitives: het bloeien, het lezen, het lopen, het spelen;
  • some words ending with aat:
    het celibaat, het commissariaat, het concordaat, het consulaat, het fabrikaat, het gelaat, het hiaat, het laminaat, het opiaat, het proletariaat, het resultaat, het secretariaat, het triumviraat, het vicariaat;
    this rule does not apply to the following nouns, which are masculine, unless noted otherwise:
    advocaat, aflaat, automaat, lidmaat (masculine / neuter), piraat, regelmaat (masculine / feminine), staat, thermostaat, vaat;
  • words ending with um:
    het arboretum, het centrum, het colloquium, het duodenum, het fatum, het herbarium, het museum, het practicum, het plectrum, het presidium, het quorum, het quotum, het sanatorium, het stadium, het terrarium;
    exception: de datum (masculine);
  • words ending with isme: het fanatisme, het fatalisme, het modernisme, het socialisme;
  • disyllabic words beginning with : ,
  • disyllabic words beginning with : ,
  • disyllabic words beginning with ont: het ontbijt, het onthaal, het ontstaan
    (de ontspanning and de ontsporing have more than two syllables, and words on ing are feminine; see below);

Names of places, i.e. cities, countries, provinces and continents, are neuter. For example: het Europa van morgen, het middeleeuwse Vlaanderen. This also affects the choice of pronouns: Europa wil zijn grenzen strenger bewaken.

However, if a country name contains a noun, the gender of the country name is determined by the gender of the noun. For example: de Chinese Volksrepubliek and de DDR (Duitse Democratische Republiek).

Names of companies are neuter, unless the name is based on a noun that is not neuter. This is reflected in the personal pronouns that are used to refer to these companies. For example, Bekaert, C&A, Ford, Lannoo and Siemens are neuter. Hence, Volkswagen voert zijn productie op.

However, the gender of company names that contain a noun is determined by the gender of that noun. For example, de Bandencentrale, de Standaard Uitgeverij and de Vlaamse Uitgeversmaatschappij.

Masculine Nouns

When a noun is masculine, this means that you refer back to it using the pronouns hij, hem and zijn.

  • male persons and animals:
    man, vader, zoon, neef, oom, dief, eunuch, pater, priester, paus, abt, ridder, verpleger, hengst, stier, reu, os, ruin, rammelaar;
  • all compounds based on words that can only refer to men:
    brandweerman, doelman, timmerman, voorman, kroegbaas, ploegbaas, burgervader, regelneef, suikeroom;
  • substantivised verb stems:
    bloei, dank, groei, schrik, slaap;
  • nouns for persons ending with aar (often names for occupations):
    ambtenaar, eigenaar, gijzelaar, goochelaar, handelaar, huichelaar, kunstenaar, leraar, leugenaar, metselaar, molenaar, moordenaar, onnozelaar, piekeraar, redenaar, schuldenaar, sjoemelaar, spijbelaar, tekenaar, tollenaar, tovenaar, twijfelaar, verzamelaar, winnaar;
    objects: korenaar;
  • nouns ending with aris (usually occupations):
    bibliothecaris, commissaris, secretaris, vicaris;
    animal: dromedaris;
  • nouns for persons ending with aard or erd (these are usually negative):
    dronkaard, koppigaard, listigaard, engerd, sufferd;
  • nouns for persons ending with der / ter (often occupations):
    boekhouder, schilder, vilder, loodgieter;
  • nouns for persons ending with er and derived from a verb stem:
    arbeider, danser, lezer, schrijver, speler, verliezer, zanger, heler;
    not derived from a verb stem: stakker;
    feminine: baker;
  • occupations and other nouns for persons ending with ent: (politie)agent, student, docent, president, dissident, regent, troonpretendent;
    this rule does not apply to concepts and objects, e.g. het reglement;
  • nouns for persons ending with ist:
    communist, economist, egoïst, egotist, filatelist, marconist, pianist, optometrist, socialist, sofist, specialist, zionist;
  • nouns for persons ending with er:
    bedrieger, kaper, kapper, leider, misdadiger, verkoper;
  • nouns for persons ending with loog:
    bioloog, geoloog, psycholoog;
  • nouns for persons ending with maan:
    bibliomaan, kleptomaan, pyromaan, sjamaan;
  • nouns for persons ending with noom:
    agronoom, econoom;
  • nouns for persons ending with teur:
    acteur, colporteur, deserteur, directeur, donateur, monteur, redacteur, saboteur, traiteur;
  • nouns for persons ending with eur:
    chauffeur, gouverneur, jongleur, programmeur, masseur, monseigneur, regisseur, souffleur;
  • nouns for persons ending with ier:
    barbier, beiaardier, herbergier, juwelier, kruidenier, marinier, musketier, pionier, pompier (Belgium), soldenier, tuinier, valkenier, vliegenier;
  • nouns for persons ending with or (as in Latin):
    consor,conservator, curator, dictator, gladiator, kolonisator, literator, mentor, professor;
    also objects ending with or:
    corridor, motor, reactor, receptor, rotor, sensor, simulator, vibrator;
  • nouns for persons ending with us (as in Latin):
    doctorandus, gladjanus, medicus, musicus, politicus;
    objects ending with us:
    catalogus, census, dinosaurus, discus, hibiscus, meniscus, muskus, syndicus, thesaurus;
    but not het circus (masculine in Latin), het corpus (neuter in Latin).
