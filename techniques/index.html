Shadowing

Shadowing is a technique formulated by Alexander Arguelles. The first links below are to Arguelles' own explanations, but others have also picked it up.

  • The Shadowing Technique and Getting Started with Shadowing.)
  • Shadowing a foreign language (Chinese), a short video that demonstrates the shadowing technique but does not explain it. (For an explanation, Arguelles refers to his website; see the above links.)
  • Shadowing Discussed (2009), a 13-minute video that comments the first video.
  • Shadowing Step by Step, a 55-minute video about the technique. Arguelles recommends that you watch his other videos about shadowing first.
  • English Speaking Practice: How to improve your English Speaking and Fluency: SHADOWING, Doing English with Julian. Julian recommends shadowing to improve your pronunciation. In the basic version of shadowing described in this video, Julian recommends that you find some audio (at your own level or slightly higher) and practise shadowing with it for an entire week. You should imitate the tone, rhythm, pauses etc. until you “become” the person you are shadowing.
  • Nagel, Donovan: Language Shadowing: A Superior Learning Method, The Mezzofanti Guild, January (?) 2013.
  • Perras, Les: should you read the script before shadowing?, a three-minute video about whether you should read the dialogue before shadowing. Les Perras provides a simple rule:
    1. Advanced students should start shadowing directly without reading the script.
    2. Beginning students should read the script once or even twice before they begin shadowing.
    However, it is a good idea to look at the script to avoid learning mistakes.
  • Huang, Angel: Boost your Chinese pronunciation & fluency with the "Character Shadowing" technique, a two-minute video that demonstrates the techniques for Standard Chinese. See also the blog post “Become” a Native Chinese Speaker with the Character Shadowing Technique, 21.04.2014.
  • Cómo mejorar la pronunciación en el aprendizaje de idiomas, a 5-minute explanation in Spanish.
  • Kaufman, Steve: The language learning technique of shadowing, an 8-minute video in which “lingosteve” explains why he does not use this technique.

Memory Palace or “Method of Loci”

The method of loci is a mnemonic technique that dates to ancient Greece and Rome. However, the only type of research it has been subjected to involved brain scans

  • Ronnie White: Mind Palace Technique From Sherlock Holmes - Memory Training: Build a Memory Palace (6 minutes). This is one of the many introductions to memory palaces that you can find on YouTube.
  • Anthony Metivier: How to Memorize the Vocabulary of Any Language Using A Memory Palace (20 minutes).
  • Anthony Metivier: 5 Ways to Ruin a Perfectly Good Memory Palace (7 minutes).
  • Anthony Metivier: How to Use Excel Files to Support Your Memory Palaces (13 minutes).
  • Evan der Millner: Memorisation using the Major Method and Method of Loci (12 minutes; Evan der Millner focuses on Latin; see latinum.org.uk).
  • Spence, Jonathan D.: The Memory Palace of Matteo Ricci. Penguin, 1985. ISBN 9780140080988. Matteo Ricci was a Jesuit priest who was sent to China in the late sixteenth century. Jonathan Spence's book describes how Ricci used memory techniques to prove the superiority of Western culture. Anthony Mettivier has a blog postThe Memory Palace of Matteo Ricci (December 2015) (and a YouTube video).
  • BrainUp: How to Memorize Chinese Characters: five-minute video that explains how to memorise Chinese characters using a memory palace.
  • Language deconstruction: a technique by Tim Ferris that helps you find out what a language is like before you start learning it. See How to Learn (But Not Master) Any Language in 1 Hour (Plus: A Favor) (7 November 2011).
  • Extensive reading (extensive reading on Wikipedia) (see also intensive reading, below).
  • Intensive reading (see also extensive reading, above).
  • Gold list “method/methodology” or Goldlist Method (by Viktor D. Huliganov; not really a method but a kind of spaced repetition).
  • 10,000 sentences: a technique formulated by Khatzumoto (which he now considers outdated) and described in the blogpost 10,000 Sentences: How (2006?).
    Note that Khatzumoto does not claim to have invented the technique. In his blogpost 10,000 Sentences: Input Before Output (2007?) he explains that the technique is based on Stephen Krashen's theory of second language acquisition (especially the input hypothesis and the importance of “comprehensible input”), and that it has been used by two Poles to learn English (see the articles Input — what it is and why you need it and How much input do you need to speak English fluently? by Tomasz P. Szynalski).

Language Learning Strategies

Language learning strategies refers to the processes and actions that are consciously deployed by language learners to help them to learn or use a language more effectively (Wikipedia).
Strategies are commonly divided into learning strategies and communicative strategies, although there are other ways of categorizing them. Learning strategies are techniques used to improve learning, such as mnemonics or using a dictionary. Communicative strategies are strategies a learner uses to convey meaning even when she doesn't have access to the correct form, such as using pro-forms like thing, or using non-verbal means such as gestures. (Wikipedia, emphasis added.)

The references below cover mostly academic resources about strategies in general.

  • Center for Advanced Research on Language Acquisition (CARLA): Research on Language Learning Strategies and Styles and Strategies-Based Language Instruction: Selected Bibliography (compiled by Andrew D. Cohen; there are a few links to downloadable PDF files).
  • Ali Cullerton: Effective Strategies for Learning a New Language (YouTube, 52 min., December 2014).
  • Language Institute, University of Wisconsin-Madison: Language Learning Strategies, a course taught to language students by Dianna Murphy in 2009:
    • Part 1: explains what language learning strategies are, asks students what strategies they use (e.g. using flashcards for vocabulary, using the language outside class, practice every day, making associations), types of strategies (metacognitive, affective and cognitive) and discusses student motivations for learning specific languages.
    • Part 2: figuring out what kind of learner you are (learning style: visual, auditory, kinesthetic), what kind of learning tasks you enjoy (depending on where you are on the introverted-extraverted continuum, on the closure-oriented versus open continuum), how you deal with new information (global versus particular), how you commit new material to memory (sharpeners versus leveler), how you deal with language rules (deductive versus inductive).
    • Part 3: the relation between learning styles and learning strategies, “effective” learning strategies (outcomes of research on good learners).
    • Part 4: continues discussion of research on good learners, and discusses the matching of strategies and tasks. (The instructor also recommends the book How to Be a More Successful Language Learner by Joan Rubin and Irene Thompseon (Heinle, 1994).
  • Anna Uhl Chamot: CALLA Content and Language Learning Strategies: a list with metacognitive strategies and task-based strategies,

Other Tips and Advice
